title: "Health Insurance and Entrepreneurship Lock"
collection: publications

excerpt: >
  Entrepreneurship lock arises when limited access to health insurance discourages self-employment. I study whether this constraint binds for young adults by exploiting the Affordable Care Act dependent coverage mandate, which extended parental plan eligibility to age 26. Using Medical Expenditure Panel Survey data from 2006–2014, I compare 19–25-year-olds to 27–34-year-olds in a difference-in-differences design. Mandate eligibility raised self-employment by 0.89 pp, a 26% increase relative to the pre-mandate treated mean. A mean-variance framework separates a subsidy channel (lower average premiums and out-of-pocket medical costs) from a risk-protection channel (the conditional second moment of total pay, proxied by total pay squared). Sanity checks show mandate effects concentrated among risk-averse young adults and those with high stated demand for insurance. Using two-stage least squares, I relate instrumented total pay and total pay squared (premiums plus out-of-pocket spending) to self-employment. Self-employment does not respond detectably to instrumented total pay, but rises when instrumented total pay squared falls, holding instrumented total pay fixed—consistent with the risk-protection channel in the mean-variance framework rather than the subsidy channel proxied by total pay alone. Distributional effects are also pronounced. The mandate expanded self-employment especially for young adults with diabetes or cancer and raised entry disproportionately among women.
