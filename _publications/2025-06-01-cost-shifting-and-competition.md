excerpt: >
  Standard models of profit-maximizing hospitals predict no cost-shifting from public to private payers. Two mechanisms overturn this result: endogenous market structure, where public reimbursement cuts induce hospital exit and raise concentration, and bilateral bargaining, where reduced public revenue shifts negotiated private prices upward. Both predict that the market power of insurers amplifies cost-shifting. Testing these predictions with Form 5500 employer health plan filings matched to NAIC Schedule T insurer data, we use staggered Medicaid expansion as a shock to the public-private payer mix. A Bartik-style shift-share instrument in an imputed difference-in-differences framework addresses the endogeneity of insurer market share. We find that market share alone has no effect on premiums, but its interaction with Medicaid expansion is large and significant: each percentage-point increase in insurer market share raises annual premium per person by approximately $36.77 in expansion states relative to non-expansion states. These results are consistent with cost-shifting operating through market concentration.
